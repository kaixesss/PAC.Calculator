Definitions

PAC Numbers are composite numbers where the digit sum of a number is equal to the digit sum of its prime factors excluding the multiplicities.

The name "PAC" came from the acronym of the last names of its founders (Polido, Antalan, and Calakhan)


Composite Numbers are numbers that has 2 or more factors excluding 1 and itself.


Prime Numbers are numbers whose factors are only 1 and itself

NOTE: "1" is not prime nor composite


Multiplicity is the degree of each factor, or the number of times a factor appears


Smith numbers are composite numbers where its digit sum is equal to the digit sum of all its prime factors. Building on this, PAC numbers share a similar foundation but differ by excluding the multiplicity of prime factors in their calculation, making them a refined version of Smith numbers.

History of PAC Numbers

The PAC number was discovered by Professor John Rafael M. Antalan and his students, Joseph Polido and John Paul V. Calakhan, at Central Luzon State University (CLSU). It evolved from the Smith number concept, which was introduced by Albert Wilansky in 1982.

Smith numbers are composite numbers where its digit sum is equal to the digit sum of all its prime factors. Building on this, PAC numbers share a similar foundation but differ by excluding the multiplicity of prime factors in their calculation, making them a refined version of Smith numbers.

This transition from Smith to PAC numbers marks a significant advancement in number theory. While both involve prime factorization, the way multiplicity is handled in PAC numbers represents a more nuanced approach. This concept has since been further developed and studied by students at Muñoz National High School Main Senior High School. Whereas STEM students; Kimm Ara Shaeenna S. Juson and Althea Marie D. Labiano, has identified the first 1000 PAC numbers, and proven its infiniteness.

Manual Computation of PAC Numbers

Below is a manual computation example for a PAC number, allowing you to verify the accuracy of the website's PAC calculator:

Example: 160

  1. Determine the prime factors of 160: 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 5 = 160, or 25 × 5.
  2. Calculate the digit sum of the prime factors, excluding multiplicity: 2 + 5 = 7.
  3. Calculate the digit sum of 160: 1 + 6 + 0 = 7.

If the digit sum of a number matches the digit sum of its prime factors, it qualifies as a PAC number. Therefore, 160 is a PAC number.
